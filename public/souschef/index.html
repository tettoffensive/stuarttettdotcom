Cooking weeknight dinners is a challenge for working professionals. Often people don't think about it until their commute home. They look in their fridge paralyzed. They often end up ordering delivery.

This gets expensive. Often times, they overlook ingredients on hand and don't use them before they spoil.

My co-founder and I wanted to see if digital technology could improve this experience. He led the development, while I led the interface and experience design.

How could technology help busy people with their weeknight meals?

The core problem is meal-planning.

Our target users have busy lives. Users need help making the time to plan and cook meals.

I began by sketching out ways a mobile app could achieve this.

If an app kept track of ingredients on hand, it could suggest more appropriate recipes.

This app could use strategic notifications to help remind busy people so everything is ready to go when they get home.

How might different users explore recipes in the app?

I talked to friends and family about their experiences with cooking recipes and recipe apps and created personas based on that research.

The biggest difference between the personas is how they prioritize time, quality, and cost.

For the Retired Schoolteacher, the most important is cost. For the Food Nerd, it's quality. And for the Working Mom, time.

How might we organize recipe details to help users to select a dish to cook?

I believe the carousel is a good balance between seeing multiple recipes at the same time and having detailed information about each recipe.

Seeing more than a photo and a title helps users make a decision quickly.

Based on user feedback from mocks, I settled on "Yuck" and "Yum" icons for influencing recipe suggestions. I removed the "Save for Later" because users thought it was redundant and confusing.

Rather than having grocery orders automatically scheduled, users felt more comfortable clicking a button to add to their shopping cart. Users can also choose to make the meal now or schedule it for another time or day.

How might we let the user explore the recipes with their own criteria?

While less discoverable, a pull–down for recipe filtering options is a great because it doesn't require taking up visual real estate with a button and other apps have set precedent for users.

I thought deeply about how filters and sorts could be applied. I came up with an interaction based on a '+' and 'x' button to toggle the effect of each option.

By simplifying down to the three factors from the personas (rating, cost, and time), I eliminated the need for filtering. Sorting by these factors meant no results are excluded.

Users felt it wasn't clear that there was a particular order to the sorts until I put everything in a vertical list and added arrows (instead of drag and drop).

Enabling and disabling sorting didn't turn out to matter so much. It's more about the order of the sorts. This simplified the interface even more and just left me with three sorts and two arrows to reorder.

How can we make entering ingredients easier?

Typing each item individually on a mobile keyboard is tedious. I wondered if there was a better way.

Using image recognition to identify a variety of foods is tough implement and error prone. But as the technology improves this could prove a convenient way to enter the data.

Unfortunately, it's not reliable enough to be the primary method of input at this time.

Another powerful data entry method would be using computer vision to read the text on receipts to identify items. This is less error prone and quite convenient when doing grocery shopping in-person.

A voice interface could be a great way to quickly enter ingredients. Items are entered one at a time, but it's faster than typing as you look around your kitchen.

Eventually smart fridges will have cameras to keep track without needing any human involved.

And since more and more shopping is done online, the app can talk directly with the online grocers to manage inventory.

After I spent some time with the Pantry as a list on a separate tab, I decided that it might be better to unify the recipes and ingredients in one screen and lose the tab bar.

In my exploration, I've learned that current technology limits what we can do to give the best experience to people. Automatic tracking just isn't there yet. It's more of an experimental features for curious users.

I'm now continuing to solve the problem by looking at how recipes can be presented to improve the cooking experience. I believe there are many improvements over existing recipe apps and books.
