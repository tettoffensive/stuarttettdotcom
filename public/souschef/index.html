In today's society, cooking week–night dinner's is a challenge. Often people don't think about it until their commute home after a long work day. They look in their fridge and are paralyzed. So they order delivery.

This gets expensive with delivery and service charges. Often times you overlook ingredients you could have used and they end up going bad when you don't use them.

How could we help people cook meals with the ingredients they have on hand?

We decided to explore using computer vision and augmented reality capabilities of the iPhone to help people manage an inventory of ingredients they have on hand.

After some early exploration with mocks in Sketch, I realized that the core problem we were trying to solve is meal-planning. It's best served as a recipe-first app, not a camera-first app.

Users want to know what they can make with the ingredients that they have. But they also want to stop throwing out food that goes bad because they don't use it.

How might different users explore recipes in the app?

After going back to the drawing board I started coming up with various the personas on friends and family who had expressed interest in the app. I chose three to work with.

Each persona has different motivations and goals for why they would use an app to find recipes.

One reason that recipe apps become so complicated is everybody has such unique requirements and tastes when it comes to food.

I thought about how each persona would use the app to select a recipe.

The biggest difference between the personas is how they want their recipes sorted and filtered as they're exploring.

While each cares about factors like time, quality, and cost, they rank them differently.

For the Retired Schoolteacher, the most important is cost. For the Food Nerd, it's quality. And for the Working Mom, time.

How might we let the user explore the recipes with their own criteria?

I explored a few variations for each of the main viewsâ€” the recipes, the ingredients pantry, and the recipe filtering.

The user is ultimately after recipes, so I chose the recipe view as the default view of the app. Once a recipe is chosen the user can press a button to "start" the recipe.

From the tab bar the user can navigate to the pantry. Here they can manually add/remove ingredients as well as search for recipes based on specific ingredients. In the future they may add ingredients by camera, smart fridge, or voice command.

Originally, I designed the filtering options so that the 'x' buttons on each option, removed that item from the list. But I wanted to avoid having another interface to add filters back to the list. So I changed it to just gray out the item to indicate a disabled state. A tap on the '+' button enables that filter again.

How can we give users a great onboarding experience with an empty ingredient list?

The first thing a user needs to suggest recipes is to input ingredients into the pantry. But this can be tedious.

We can improve this process a bit by automatically adding common ingredients to the list that almost everyone has like salt, pepper, olive oil, water, etc. The user can confirm or deny any of these items during onboarding since nothing is a guarantee.

How can we make entering ingredients painless?

This is where the computer vision comes in. This is difficult to implement and error prone. But as the model improves this could prove a convenient way to enter the data.

Eventually, this work would be taken over by a smart fridge with cameras to keep track without needing any human involved.

In the meantime, a more convenient method would be for the user to speak into the phone's microphone and convert that speech to text to be entered into the database.

Even if there are mistakes, the corrections would be significantly easier than to manually enter all of the ingredients.
