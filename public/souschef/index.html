In today's society, cooking week–night dinner's is a challenge. Often people don't think about it until their commute home after a long work day. They look in their fridge and are paralyzed. So they order delivery.

This gets expensive with delivery and service charges. Often times you overlook ingredients you could have used and they end up going bad when you don't use them.

How could we help people cook meals with the ingredients they have on hand?

We decided to explore using computer vision and augmented reality capabilities of the iPhone to help people manage an inventory of ingredients they have on hand.

After some early exploration with mocks in Sketch, I realized that the core problem we were trying to solve is meal-planning. It's best served as a recipe-first app, not a camera-first app.

Users want to know what they can make with the ingredients that they have. But they also want to stop throwing out food that goes bad because they don't use it.

I went back to the drawing board and chose 3 user personas to explore.

I based the personas on friends and family who had expressed interest in the app. The main difference between the personas is how they want the suggested recipes sorted.

Each persona prioritizes different things in the meals they want to cook.

I then explored a few variations for each of the main viewsâ€” the recipes, the ingredients pantry, and the recipe filtering.

I decided that the home base of the app will be the recipes view. This is ultimately what the user is after most of the time. Other functions and features are secondary. Once a recipe is chosen the user can press a button to "start" the recipe.

From the tab bar the user can navigate to the pantry. Here they can manually add/remove ingredients as well as search for recipes based on specific ingredients. In the future they may add ingredients by camera, smart fridge, or voice command.

Originally, I designed the filtering options so that the 'x' buttons on each option, removed that item from the list. I realized that for adding a filter option back to the list another interface would be needed. Instead I changed it to just gray out the item on 'x' and place it at the end. A tap on the '+' button enables that option again.

I thought about how the user can get to what they are after—the recipes.

Recipes with ingredients you have on hand aren't available unless the app knows what ingredients you have on hand ;) So the first thing to do is ask the user to enter some ingredients.

We can improve this process a bit by automatically adding common ingredients to the list that almost everyone has like salt, pepper, olive oil, water. The user can confirm or deny any of these items during onboarding since nothing's a guarantee

Having a set of common ingredients, helps the app at least return some results. But they're not good results.

It's better for the app to just return popular recipes in the case that the pantry is empty or doesn't have enough ingredients to make any recipe suggestions relevant.

We also need an effecient way for the user to do simple data-entry for the pantry.

In it's conception the idea was to scan your kitchen with the phone and the items would be recognized by the app. This is difficult to implement and error prone. But as the model improves this could prove a convenient way to enter the data.

Eventually, this work would be taken over by a smart fridge with cameras or other technology to recognize the ingredients.

In the meantime, typing on a mobile keyboard all the ingredients you have is rather tedious. I think a superior method would be for the user to speak into the phone's microphone and convert that speech to text to be entered into the database.

Even if there are mistakes, the corrections would be significantly easier than to manually enter all of the ingredients.

My next steps are to look at the experiences of:

  • What happens when the user decides to cook a particular recipe?
  • How does the app keep track of used up or expired ingredients? (I have found a database of how long certain foods last)
  • How can we use notifications intelligently so that the user can plan meals ahead of time? The user likely needs to order some ingredients from a grocery delivery service. This can take a couple hours. My wife an I often don't think of what to cook until we leave the office.
